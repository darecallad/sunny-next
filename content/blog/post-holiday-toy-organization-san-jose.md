The wrapping paper is cleared away. The turkey leftovers are in the fridge. But there is one thing remaining: The Toys.

If your **San Jose** living room looks like a toy store exploded, you are not alone. It’s the "Day After Christmas" reality. While we are grateful for the generosity of friends and family, the sheer volume of *stuff* can be overwhelming—for you, and surprisingly, for your toddler too.

When a child has too many options, they often play *less*. They dump everything out, get overwhelmed, and walk away (leaving you to clean up).

Here are three steps to tame the "Toy Tsunami" and reclaim your home.

### 1. The "Quarantine" Box
Before you try to find a place for every new toy, grab a big plastic bin or a cardboard box.
Put at least 50% of the new toys directly into this box.
Put it in the garage or a high closet.

**Why?**
Toddlers can't appreciate 10 new toys at once. By hiding half of them, you are saving them for a rainy day in February when you really need a distraction. We call this "Toy Rotation," and it keeps toys feeling fresh and exciting for months.

### 2. Categorize, Don't Just Dump
We often use big toy chests where everything gets thrown in together. This is a recipe for chaos.
Instead, use smaller, open bins or baskets.
*   **Bin A:** Cars and trucks.
*   **Bin B:** Blocks.
*   **Bin C:** Soft animals.

When toys have a specific "home," clean-up time becomes a matching game. "Can you find the home for the red truck?" is much easier for a toddler to understand than "Clean up your room."

### 3. The "One In, One Out" Ritual
This is a great habit to start early.
For every new toy that stays in the living room, find an old toy to "retire."
You can involve your child in this (if they are old enough), or do it while they sleep (if they are younger).
*   **Broken toys:** Trash/Recycle.
*   **Outgrown toys:** Donate to a local **San Jose** charity or save for a younger sibling.

### Less is More
A calm, organized environment leads to calmer, more focused play. By reducing the clutter, you aren't being "mean"—you are creating a space where your child's imagination can actually breathe.

Good luck with the clean-up! You've got this.

包裝紙都清掉了。火雞剩菜都在冰箱裡了。但還有一樣東西留了下來：玩具。

如果你在 **San Jose** 的客廳看起來像是玩具店爆炸現場，你並不孤單。這就是「聖誕節隔天」的現實。雖然我們很感謝親朋好友的慷慨，但這些*東西*的數量真的會讓人崩潰——對你來說是這樣，令人驚訝的是，對你的幼兒來說也是。

當孩子有太多選擇時，他們通常玩得*更少*。他們會把所有東西倒出來，感到不知所措，然後走開（留給你去收拾）。

這裡有三個步驟，幫你馴服這場「玩具海嘯」，奪回你的家。

### 1. 「隔離」箱
在你試著為每一個新玩具找位子之前，拿一個大的塑膠箱或紙箱。
把至少 50% 的新玩具直接放進這個箱子裡。
把它放到車庫或高處的櫃子裡。

**為什麼？**
幼兒無法同時欣賞 10 個新玩具。把一半藏起來，你是為了二月的某個下雨天、當你真的需要轉移他們注意力時而存的。我們稱之為「玩具輪替 (Toy Rotation)」，這能讓玩具在好幾個月裡都保持新鮮感和吸引力。

### 2. 分類，不要只是丟進去
我們常使用那種大大的玩具箱，把所有東西都丟在一起。這是製造混亂的配方。
相反地，使用較小的、開放式的籃子或盒子。
*   **A 籃：** 車子和卡車。
*   **B 籃：** 積木。
*   **C 籃：** 布偶。

當玩具有一個特定的「家」時，收拾時間就變成了配對遊戲。「你能幫紅色卡車找到它的家嗎？」這比「去整理房間」更容易讓幼兒理解。

### 3. 「一進一出」的儀式
這是一個很值得從小養成的習慣。
每有一個新玩具留在客廳，就找一個舊玩具「退休」。
你可以讓孩子參與這個過程（如果他們夠大的話），或是趁他們睡覺時做（如果他們還小）。
*   **壞掉的玩具：** 丟棄/回收。
*   **長大不玩的玩具：** 捐給 **San Jose** 當地的慈善機構，或是留給弟弟妹妹。

### 少即是多
一個平靜、有條理的環境能帶來更冷靜、更專注的遊戲品質。減少雜物並不是「小氣」——你是在創造一個讓孩子的想像力能夠呼吸的空間。

祝你收拾順利！你可以的。
