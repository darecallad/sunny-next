It's the moment every parent dreads. The family is gathered in **San Jose**. The camera phones are out. Grandma hands your 3-year-old a beautifully wrapped box. They rip it open, look at the hand-knitted sweater inside, and scream:

*"NO! I wanted the robot!"*

Or worse, they just toss it aside and reach for the next one.

You want to melt into the floor. You feel like everyone is judging your parenting. But before you apologize profusely or scold your child, take a breath.

Toddlers are not rude; they are unfiltered. Their brains haven't developed the social nuance to say a "white lie." If they feel disappointed, they show it.

Here is how to prepare for (and handle) the gift-opening frenzy.

### 1. Role-Play the "Thank You" Face
Toddlers learn by doing. A few days before Christmas, practice with a "pretend" gift (use a sock or a spoon wrapped in a towel).

**The Game:** "We are going to practice our Happy Thank You Face! No matter what is inside, we smile and say 'Thank You!'"
Make it silly. Wrap up a banana. Wrap up a diaper. When they open it and smile, cheer for them! This builds muscle memory for the big day.

### 2. Focus on the Giver, Not the Gift
When a child is disappointed by the *object*, redirect their attention to the *person*.

If they say, "I don't like this," validate them quietly ("I know you wanted a toy"), but then say aloud:
*"Wow, Grandma spent so much time picking this just for you because she loves you. Let's say 'Thank you, Grandma' for thinking of us."*
This shifts the value from the material item to the relationship.

### 3. Slow the Pace (The "One-at-a-Time" Rule)
Overstimulation is the enemy of gratitude. When a child sees a pile of 10 gifts, they go into a frenzy. They stop seeing the gifts and just want the dopamine hit of ripping paper.

**The Strategy:** Hand out one gift at a time.
Do not let them open the next one until they have looked at the current one, said thank you, and maybe even played with it for a minute. Slowing down helps them process what is happening and reduces the "Gimme More" meltdown.

### Grace Over Perfection
If your child does say something embarrassing, don't shame them in front of everyone. A simple, "We're still learning how to be polite," to the adults is enough.

The holidays are a learning curve for everyone. Keep it light, keep it slow, and remember: their honesty is actually a sign that they feel safe with you.

這是每個父母都害怕的時刻。全家人聚集在 **San Jose**。手機相機都準備好了。奶奶遞給你 3 歲的孩子一個包裝精美的盒子。他們撕開包裝，看著裡面的手織毛衣，然後大叫：

*「不要！我想要機器人！」*

或者更糟，他們直接把它丟在一邊，伸手去拿這下一個。

你恨不得找個地洞鑽進去。你覺得大家都在評判你的教養。但在你拼命道歉或責罵孩子之前，深呼吸。

幼兒不是沒禮貌；他們只是「沒有過濾器」。他們的大腦還沒發展出說「善意謊言」的社交細微差別。如果他們感到失望，他們就會表現出來。

以下是如何準備（並應對）這場拆禮物的混亂。

### 1. 角色扮演「謝謝臉」
幼兒是透過實作來學習的。在聖誕節前幾天，用一個「假」禮物（用毛巾包一隻襪子或湯匙）來練習。

**遊戲規則：** 「我們要來練習我們的『快樂謝謝臉』！不管裡面是什麼，我們都要微笑說『謝謝』！」
讓過程變得好玩。包一根香蕉。包一片尿布。當他們打開並微笑時，為他們歡呼！這能為大日子的到來建立肌肉記憶。

### 2. 專注於「送禮者」，而不是「禮物」
當孩子對*物品*感到失望時，將他們的注意力轉移到*人*身上。

如果他們說：「我不喜歡這個」，輕聲同理他們（「我知道你想要玩具」），然後大聲說：
*「哇，奶奶花了好多時間特別為你挑選這個，因為她很愛你。我們跟奶奶說『謝謝奶奶』想著我們。」*
這將價值從物質物品轉移到了人際關係上。

### 3. 放慢速度（「一次一個」法則）
過度刺激是感恩的敵人。當孩子看到一堆 10 個禮物時，他們會陷入瘋狂。他們不再看見禮物，只想追求撕開包裝紙的那種多巴胺快感。

**策略：** 一次只給一個禮物。
在他們看過目前的禮物、說了謝謝，甚至玩了一分鐘之前，不要讓他們開下一個。放慢速度有助於他們消化正在發生的事情，並減少「我還要更多」的崩潰。

### 優雅勝過完美
如果你的孩子真的說了什麼尷尬的話，不要在大家面前羞辱他們。對大人們簡單說一句：「我們還在學習禮貌」，就足夠了。

節日對每個人來說都是一個學習曲線。保持輕鬆，放慢腳步，並記住：他們的誠實其實代表他們在你身邊感到很安全。
